What I heard

  • Your North American motion is active but not yet focused. SDRs are targeting commercial landlords across all asset classes using a broad CRE fit score, and the numbers reflect it: outbound win rate sits at 2.8% against an inbound rate of 25.6%. The issue is not the product. It is the targeting.
  • You have the data to solve this. Your FY26 Salesforce pipeline shows clear patterns: commercial property managers using QuickBooks Online win at 56%, those using NetSuite win at 53%, and deals with no accounting stack on record win at 0%. The quantitative case for focusing on specific customer segments is already there. The qualitative picture, from customers, lost deals, and the people closest to the sales conversations, will complete it. Together, they turn observation into a defensible decision.
  • There is significant unrealised pipeline sitting idle. 75 postponed deals carry NZD $1.96M in potential ARR. That pipeline has not been re-engaged with any segment-specific rationale.
  • North America is an early-stage market for Re-Leased, and the global brand has been doing a lot of the work so far. There is now an opportunity to build something more deliberate for the region: a focused view of which customers to pursue, localised messaging, and a channel approach built around how NA commercial property managers actually buy. Jana has been building the conviction internally to make this happen. This engagement gives that conviction a commercial foundation.

The opportunity

Re-Leased already knows who it wins with. The task is not discovery. It is commitment. Companies that get clear on their best-fit customer before scaling their sales motion consistently see win rates improve by 15 to 25 percentage points within two quarters, because every part of the motion, targeting, message, objection handling, and follow-up, is built for the same buyer. Your UK business at NZD $27K average ARR versus your NA business at NZD $9K tells the story clearly: the UK has focus, and NA does not yet. Closing that gap is the single highest-leverage move available to Re-Leased in North America right now.

How I work

  1. Start with the mandate, not the channel. Strategy before tactics. The answer to "which channels should we use" comes after "who are we going after and why." Not before.
  2. Eliminate before you invest. Most pipeline problems are targeting problems. The fastest way to find your best customer is to get clear on who is not a fit. Ruling out the wrong buyers makes the right ones obvious, and it makes every dollar spent on reaching them go further.
  3. Fix the message before scaling spend. More outbound on a weak message creates more noise. The right message to the right buyer in the right channel is where the work starts.
  4. Own pipeline outcomes, not activity metrics. Marketing is accountable for pipeline quality and CAC payback, not calls made or emails sent. The measure of this engagement is what changes in the quality of what goes into the funnel.
  5. Build frameworks that last. A campaign ends. A well-built qualification framework and messaging hierarchy stays in the business and keeps paying. That is what gets built here.

What I need to do this well

Good segmentation work is only as strong as the inputs. To deliver the outcomes above, I will need the following from the Re-Leased team.

  • Post-sales data by segment. NRR by segment, churn by segment, average deal cycle by segment, and customer cost data. This is the quantitative foundation for the segment hypothesis work in Weeks 1 and 2.
  • Current CRE fit score and SDR targeting criteria. Understanding how the team is currently qualifying and scoring leads is essential before we redefine those criteria.
  • Customer conversations. Warm introductions or direct contact details for two to three current customers per priority segment. Interview format, 30 to 45 minutes each. These validate the quantitative picture with real-world experience.
  • Lost deal conversations. Contact details or warm introductions to two to three prospects who evaluated Re-Leased and chose not to proceed. These conversations are often the most valuable of all.
  • A customer experience representative. One CX or customer success person for a 45-minute conversation about what strong customers look like post-sale and where friction tends to appear.
  • A session with Jana. One working session focused on the current NA marketing plan: channels tested, expected spend, what has worked so far, and lead volumes across different segments. This shapes the GTM plan in the final phase.
  • 45 minutes with Dai. An interview-style conversation in Week 1 to understand the commercial priorities, the constraints, and what success looks like from a leadership perspective.
