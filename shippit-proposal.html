Where this fits, depending on what you decide

You are still working through which path is right. This engagement adds value under all three. Here is how.

Path 1

You hire a strategic CMO

Any CMO worth hiring will want to assess the business before acting. That ramp costs time. This sprint gives them a clean starting point: tight ICP, clear narrative, 90-day plan ready to pressure-test. Less ramp, faster action from day one.

The sprint becomes the brief the new hire inherits, not a problem they have to solve on the clock.

Path 2

You backfill a Head of Marketing operator

If the next person is a strong executor without a strategic layer, they need CMO-level thinking behind them or the same gap reappears. This work provides the positioning, ICP, and GTM architecture so your operator has something solid to run against from day one.

CMO-level foundations without a CMO salary. Clean handover to the incoming operator.

Path 3

You bring in a CRO or GTM leader

A CRO across sales and marketing needs the marketing foundations in good shape to orchestrate a joined-up revenue motion. If narrative and ICP are unclear, even a strong CRO spends their first months diagnosing instead of leading.

Clean foundations mean the CRO leads from day one. The hardest problem is already solved before they arrive.

My honest view

Based on what you shared, I think Path 2 is where you are heading. A strong operator as Head of Marketing, with the strategic foundations set first so they have something to run against. The narrative work and ICP clarity are too important to leave to an incoming hire to figure out on the clock. Getting that done now means whoever you bring in starts from a position of strength, not a blank page. If Path 3 is where you land, the same logic applies. Either way, the foundations sprint is the right first move.
